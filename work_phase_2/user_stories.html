User Stories

Aisha

Aisha is 35 years old. She is from Syria and has just immigrated to Australia.Aisha shared that she faces many difficulties when trying to find online support as a newcomer. Her biggest challenges are language barriers and overwhelming information. Many websites only partially support Arabic, making her feel confused and anxious. She emphasized the need for websites to have a clean layout, visual guidance (icons/images), and compatibility with slow internet.

1. Can you describe your experience trying to find online services that support migrants like yourself?

Yes… it was hard.

  • When I first came to Australia, I needed to find help for many things—healthcare, English classes, how to rent a home. I searched online, but many websites were confusing.There were too many words, and I didn’t know which one to trust. Sometimes, I didn’t even know the right words to type into Google. I asked my son to help me, but he is also still learning.

2. What challenges have you faced when looking for help online in Australia?

I think the biggest challenge is language…

  • Even if a website has Arabic, it’s not always full translation.Some buttons or pages stay in English. Also, websites have too much information at once. I get lost. Some ask me to fill forms, but I don’t know what they mean. I worry I will do something wrong. Also, sometimes websites don’t work well on my phone. I don’t have a computer, only my phone.

3. If your internet connection was slow or your English was limited, how would that affect your experience?

Very difficult.

  • When internet is slow, big websites take long to load. Sometimes they don’t open. And if the English is too difficult, I just give up. I feel tired and stressed. I would rather go to the community centre and ask in person, but not always possible. So I need websites that are easy and work fast, even with slow internet.

David

David is a 28-year-old recent graduate who just launched his tech startup. He is actively seeking digital tools to promote his services, attract clients, and build credibility online. In the interview, David shared that he currently uses platforms like LinkedIn, Instagram, and basic website builders (e.g., Wix or Squarespace), but finds them limited in terms of business-specific features such as advanced SEO tools, analytics, and client communication integrations.

1. What kind of online tools or platforms are you currently using to promote your startup?

  • Right now, I’m using LinkedIn, Instagram, and a Wix website I built myself. They help me get some exposure, but they feel limited. I’ve tried using Google Ads too, but without solid SEO, it’s hard to get organic traffic. I also sometimes post on startup communities like Product Hunt or Reddit, but it’s a lot to manage.

2. What makes a website appealing or trustworthy for your potential customers?

  • It has to look clean, professional, and fast. First impressions matter—a cluttered or outdated website makes people bounce. Also, people look for clear descriptions of services, maybe testimonials, and a simple way to contact us. If I can show credibility and make it easy for clients to understand what I offer, that’s a win.

3. Tell me what features or content you would expect on a site that supports startups.

  • SEO tools to get found
  • Contact forms, chatbots, or booking systems
  • A clean portfolio layout
  • Integration with analytics (like GoogleAnalytics)
  • Blog/news section for updates
  • Testimonial or case study areas
  • Also, maybe access to startup resources or a community space—like a dashboard for early founders.

Lily

As an international student living in Australia, I want a trustworthy and mobile-friendly website that helps me find housing and jobs with easy-to-understand guides, so that I can avoid scams, understand my rights, and settle into life here more smoothly even with limited time or language confidence.

1. What has been your experience trying to find information online about housing or jobs in Australia?

  • It’s been a bit stressful. When I first arrived, I needed to find a place to live and a part-time job quickly. I searched online a lot—Facebook groups, job boards, and university pages—but it was all spread out. Some info was outdated, and I wasn’t sure which websites were trustworthy. Also, there were scams, especially in housing. I felt nervous contacting strangers.

2. Can you give an example of a time a website helped you (or didn’t)?

  • The university job board helped me find my first part-time job on campus. It was easy to understand, and they explained the pay rate and work rules clearly. But when I tried to use a job site like Seek, it was overwhelming—too many filters, and I didn’t know which roles were okay for student visas. I gave up and asked my friends for help instead.

3. If you were only using your phone, what would make the website easier to use?

  • It needs to be mobile-friendly, with big buttons, less text, and maybe step-by-step pages. ’d like to see maps, maybe quick translation options, or even a chatbot for help. On the phone, everything should fit well on the screen—no zooming in and out.
